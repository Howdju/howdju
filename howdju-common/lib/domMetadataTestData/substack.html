RFK Jr. Is Striking a Nerve. He Tells Me Why.

On the latest episode of Honestly, I spoke to the presidential candidate about why he’s polling at 20 percent among likely Democrats.

By Bari Weiss

June 21, 2023

  • Like
  • Comment
  • Share

A few days ago, Joe Rogan offered Dr. Peter Hotez $100,000 to appear on his show to debate RFK Jr. on the subject of vaccines and public health. Not 48 hours later, thanks to Twitter, the ante is now more than $2 million and counting.

If this debate happens, it could rival the audience tuning in to the official presidential debates in the 2024 election cycle. 

Which tells us a tremendous amount about our current political moment.

So does the fact that Robert Francis Kennedy Jr. is even running for president.

He is the rare Kennedy who, until now, hasn’t joined the family business. At age 69, he has had a long career as an environmental lawyer and activist—protecting the Hudson River, founding the biggest network of water protection organizations in the world, fighting legal battles against polluters, and supporting initiatives to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. Now Kennedy lives in a beautiful neighborhood in L.A. with his wife, the Curb Your Enthusiasm actress Cheryl Hines. They have a combined seven children. He hikes every day. It seems like they have a good, very comfortable life.

That’s one version of Kennedy’s biography.

The other side of the story is that he used to be a heroin addict (he’s now sober). He’s had affairs; as he once wrote in a leaked diary a decade ago, “his lust demons” are his greatest defect. And most notably, his interest in public health does not end with the environment. Kennedy has taken positions—namely his belief that childhood vaccines are the reason for the precipitous rise in autism—that most scientists, and several members of his own family, say are dangerous and disqualifying. 

Given all that, we might not expect that Kennedy would decide to run for anything, let alone for president of the United States. But here he is seeking office for the very first time. (Read our own Peter Savodnik’s profile of RFK Jr. here.)

Many voices in the mainstream—or rather, what used to be the mainstream—dismiss RFK Jr. as a distraction. The New York Times recently called him a “crank” and “a high-profile circus act.”

But if we look at the polls—if we look at what Americans actually think—they don’t seem to agree.

RFK Jr. is polling as high as 20 percent among Democratic voters. According to one poll from The Economist and YouGov, RFK Jr. has the highest favorability rating among all major candidates, including Biden and Trump. 

Though a challenger to the incumbent has never won the primaries in modern political history—and Kennedy doesn’t seem poised to break that historical precedent—that he’s doing this well so early in the race tells us a lot about where we are in 2023. Namely, that voters are deeply dissatisfied with the options on the table—especially Democrats, who are desperate for a Biden alternative. According to one recent poll, 4 in 10 Dems want Biden to step aside in 2024. 

I also think it tells us something much deeper about what’s happening in American life that goes far beyond partisan politics.

Which brings us back to Rogan’s debate offer.

At the heart of the currently unfolding Twitterstorm is not the question of whether Hotez or another doctor will appear on Rogan’s podcast for a debate. (Though for my money it should be Dr. Vinay Prasad, who has a piece about Kennedy’s public health views today in The Free Press that you should read.)

It’s the far more existential question about who Americans really trust these days. We are living in a country in which many Americans put more faith in Rogan more than they do in The New York Times or the CDC. It is essential to understand the reasons why. 

Kennedy’s popularity also speaks to the question of what (and who) ought to fit inside the realm of acceptable debate in our culture and whether the powers that be have drawn the lines too narrowly. (If I didn’t believe they had, The Free Press would not exist.)

So I find myself of two minds when it comes to Kennedy. When I hear him speak about regulatory capture or the cozy relationship between Big Tech and the government or the censoriousness of our gatekeepers, I find myself nodding along. 

But just because some conspiracy theories turn out to be true (see under: lab leak and Hunter Biden laptop) doesn’t mean they all are. 

The vaccines that eradicated diseases like polio and measles are a modern miracle. So when I hear Kennedy insisting that vaccinations are dangerous and are the reason for the rise in autism (an unsubstantiated view he promotes through his nonprofit, the Children’s Health Defense Fund); or suggesting that antidepressants are a cause of school shootings, he loses me. (Then there is his apparent admiration for Hugo Chavez.)

Yet millions of people are willing and eager to entertain such views. Or at the very least, they are willing to overlook them because they are compelled by a candidate who has the courage of his convictions and is willing to buck groupthink. 

Can the legacy press just wish RFK Jr. away? I don’t think so. And I think it’s absolutely foolish to try.

Think back to June 2015. Trump was polling at around 11 percent. One lesson of the past decade of American politics is that the pundits and the experts are so often wrong. More to the point, it is the fundamental job of journalists to understand why a man like Kennedy has hit such a nerve.

So last week, I went to Kennedy’s house to ask him why.

I’d love for you to listen to our conversation and to read Dr. Prasad. Unlike me, he has a degree in epidemiology and he’s far better situated to assess where RFK Jr. gets it right—and where he gets it wrong.

See you in the comments.

—BW
